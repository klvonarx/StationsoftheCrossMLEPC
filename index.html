The Scriptural Stations

This form of the Stations of the Cross draws every station directly from the Gospels. There are no traditions or legends — only scripture. Every moment you will reflect on is something the Gospel writers recorded.

These stations were prayed publicly by Pope John Paul II on Good Friday 1991 and have become a beloved alternative to the traditional 14 stations. The USCCB presents them as a way of entering more deeply into the biblical account of Christ's passion.

They begin not at Pilate's courtyard but in the Garden of Gethsemane — with Jesus in anguish, praying alone. From there, the story unfolds exactly as the Gospels tell it.

How this differs from the traditional stations

The traditional 14 stations include several moments not found in the Gospels — Jesus falling three times, Veronica wiping his face, Mary meeting him on the road. These come from later Catholic tradition and devotion, not directly from scripture.

The Scriptural Stations replace those with moments that are in the Bible but often overlooked in prayer: Jesus in Gethsemane, Judas' betrayal with a kiss, Peter's denial in the courtyard, and Jesus promising paradise to the thief hanging beside him.

The result is a version that stays tightly with the Gospel narrative — powerful for anyone who wants to walk through the Passion story as the four evangelists actually wrote it.

How to pray these stations

At each station, begin with the traditional response: "We adore you, O Christ, and we bless you / Because by your holy cross you have redeemed the world."

Then listen to the scripture reading. Let the words land. Don't rush to the reflection — sit in the silence first. Then read the reflection, sit with the question, and pray.

After each station, the traditional closing response is: "Lord Jesus, help us walk in your steps."

The stations close at the tomb. We pray them always knowing what comes next.
